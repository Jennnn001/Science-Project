Remote Sensing

Remote sensing is the process of gathering information about an object or area from a distance, typically using satellites, drones, or aircraft. It involves capturing electromagnetic radiation reflected or emitted by the Earth's surface. Remote sensing is classified based on the source of radiation. The two main types are:

01. Passive Remote Sensing

Passive Remote Sensing is a type of remote sensing that relies on natural sources of radiation, mainly from the Sun. It detects reflected or emitted energy from Earth's surface, thus it is very common in optical and thermal imaging.

02. Active Remote Sensing

Active Remote Sensing is a type of remote sensing that relies on artificial sources of radiation, such as radar or laser. It emits signals and analyzes the reflected energy. Unlike passive remote sensing, it is able to work at night and day, even in cloudy conditions.

TRACKING POLLUTION

Remote sensing plays a crucial role in tracking deforestation and pollution by using satellite imagery and airborne sensors to monitor environmental changes over time. By capturing differences in land cover, it can detect areas where forests have been cleared, helping identify deforestation patterns. In addition to deforestation, remote sensing detects pollution in soil, air, and water by analyzing chemical composition, moisture levels, and atmospheric changes. It can identify soil contamination from mining and agriculture, and detecting harmful gases released from natural and human activities.

LANDSTAT

Uses satellite imaging to capture detailed views of Earth's surface every 16 days. It helps monitor deforestation through remote sensing by detecting changes in forest cover, land use, and logging activity over time.

TROPOMI

Using remote sensing, it tracks air pollution by measuring gases like nitrogen dioxide and carbon monoxide from space, allowing scientists to detect pollution sources and monitor atmospheric health.

Remote Sensing - What Could be Seen?

Change in Color
In before images, the rainforest appears dark green, showing healthy vegetation. However, In after images, deforested areas appear light brown or tan, indicating bare soil or farmland.
Vegetation Density
Normalized Difference Vegetation Index data, which measures vegetation health and density using satellite imagery, shows a significant drop in vegetation health, confirming deforestation
Fire Observed
Fires resulting from land clearing activities are clearly visible in infrared satellite imagery, appearing as distinct red or orange heat spots that indicate intense thermal activity.
